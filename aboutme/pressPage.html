About

Virus, The Beauty of the Beast is an interactive documentary about viruses, and about their connections to human designs, based on actual scientific models.

Why did I make The Beauty of the Beast?

[Target audience is journalists. We can assume they have played the thing]

It actually started out with me just wanting to make an enjoyable video game!

Like a lot of people of my generation, I feel that video games, the entertainment medium that I grew up with, have had their potential squandered. For me, good video games give us insights into rules and systems - that is something that could be used to make profound and world-changing things, but only a few games have done this. The big ones for me are Portal and Braid, both of which can be enjoyed by anyone, and both of which have mechanics and puzzle design inspired by incredible pieces of science (respectively, wormholes and non-linear time).

Between 2012 and 2014, I worked in the games industry; but never anything that was scientific in an uncompromising way. So eventually I decided to make a prototype “interactive documentary”, called The Stranger Loop, inspired by the game Asteroids. A lot of people liked The Stranger Loop, which proved to me that “interactive documentaries” could be a good idea.

But what about the virus patterns? I originally encountered them while studying mathematics during my undergrad. They amazed me. I decided I wanted to move towards biology for my career; I put the Islamic patterns I learned about from the course into the backgrounds of my first game, Music of the Spheres; and then, in 2014, I decided to make a full game about how beautiful viruses were. I would combine it with the “interactive documentary” idea I had thought about before, and I would use everything I knew about video games to make it as fun as possible.

I expected the project to take 10 months. It has now taken three years and cost £2500 (professional shooting and illustrations). I was occasionally full-time on it, but mostly part-time while I’ve worked in schools and on scientific projects. At the start I wasn't very good at presenting, so I ended up re-writing and re-shooting the whole thing six times. I had also never done 3D programming before I began it - to make the first version (below) I had to teach myself openGL - but then I ended up having to scrap all that work in favour of webGL anyway - which I also had to teach myself!

Only through interactive entertainment -video games- can you get real insights into biological systems. And though The Beauty of the Beast may not seem very much like a video game, to me, that is what it is.

The Science of the three models

The "hexagons and pentagons" model describing Hepatitis, discovered in 1960, is one of the most important models in all of structural biology. It describes the majority of viruses (and since viruses are 90% of living things, it describes the majority of living things!). Almost every new discovery regarding virus structure is seen in light of this model. One of the largest virus information databases, viperDB, partly organizes their data around this model.

The "Islamic art" model that describes Zika virus and HPV is more of a wildcard. It was published extremely recently (2014). It has been discussed, and the mathematics all works out, but there are a few experiments that still need to be done with it. The Islamic art connection is roundabout but very real.

The "corner moving" model describing HIV is a new model developed as part of my Master's dissertation. Its interest is mostly mathematical - IT IS NOT LIKELY AT ALL TO LEAD TO A CURE FOR HIV! There are other means of modelling viruses shells like HIV's which have been used. However, it is definitely simpler than any previous model and does enable some new things, so it may turn out to be useful for modelling other viruses. Work on it has not yet been peer-reviewed, but it is known to be compatible with all existing data on virus structure.
