About

Virus, The Beauty of the Beast is an interactive documentary about viruses, and about their connections to human designs. Throughout it, the audience can interact with actual scientific models of viruses.

Who am I?
My name is Hamish Todd, and I'm a computational biology PhD student working at the universities of Edinburgh and Oxford. Why did I make The Beauty of the Beast?

I'm part of group that is trying to change education by making "explorable explanations", which aim to let laypeople play with the models that scientists have been able to play with since the computer was invented - but that's not how it started. It honestly started out with me just wanting to make a fun video game!

Like a lot of people of my generation, I feel that video games, the entertainment medium I have grown up with, has had its potential squandered (only a few games, like Portal and Braid, have done anything with that potential). Between 2012 and 2014, I worked in the games industry, releasing things independently and odd design jobs (I had some levels in a Hello Kitty game). But I wanted to make something uncompromisingly scientific. That lead to me making, in seven days, a prototype called The Stranger Loop. A lot of people liked it, which proved to me that “interactive documentaries” could be a good idea.

And what about the virus patterns? I originally encountered them while studying mathematics during my undergrad. They amazed me. I decided I wanted to move towards biology for my career; I put the Islamic patterns I learned about from the course into the backgrounds of my first game, Music of the Spheres; and then, in 2014, I decided to make a full game about how beautiful viruses were. I would combine it with the “interactive documentary” idea I had thought about before, and I would use everything I knew about video games to make it as fun as possible.

I expected the project to take 10 months. It has now taken three years. I was occasionally full-time on it, but mostly part-time while I’ve worked in schools and on other scientific projects. At the start I wasn't very good at presenting, so I ended up re-writing and re-shooting the whole thing six times. I had also never done 3D programming before I began it.

Only through interactive entertainment -video games- can you get real insights into biological systems. And though The Beauty of the Beast may not seem very much like a video game, to me, that is what it is.

The Science of the three models

The "hexagons and pentagons" model describing Hepatitis, discovered in 1960, goes by the name of "Caspar Klug theory" and is one of the most important theories in all of structural biology. It describes the majority of viruses (and since viruses are 90% of living things, it describes the majority of living things!). Almost every new discovery regarding virus structure is seen in light of this model. One of the largest virus information databases, viperDB, partly organizes their data around this model.

The "corner moving" model describing HIV is a new model developed as part of my Master's dissertation. To be clear: it is *not at all* going to lead to a cure for HIV, and work on it has not yet been peer-reviewed. But we do expect it to be useful for modelling viruses with shells like HIV.

The "Islamic art" model that describes Zika virus and HPV is more of a wildcard. It was published extremely recently (2015), by Olga Konevtsova (Physics of the Solid State 57.4). Is it a completely reliable model? We aren't sure - we don't have enough data at the moment. But whatever we find, the connection between Islamic art and HPV isn't going to go away. Other work on HPV (Twarock 2004, Klug 1990), via the "Penrose tiling", connects it to something that the architects of the Darb-I imam shrine are believed to have been considering (Lu, 2007)

For more details, contact me! There is a lot of information substantiating the connections I make further which I left out
